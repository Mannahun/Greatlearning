The elbow method is frequently used to determine the optimal number of clusters for K means clustering, as a plot between number of clusters and the distortion, defined as the average of the squared distances from the cluster centers of the respective clusters. K means is an algorithm that requires the operator to input the number of clusters. This is going to affect the final results, because data are then divided up in clusters. In this case, there seems to be an 'elbow' or a change in the cure, with 3 clusters. 3 clusters were therefore used for the subsequent analysis.

Unlike K Means, hierarchy clustering does not require the user to provide the number of clusters, so, in a sense, it may represent a less-biased approach to clustering, because it is not dependent from a user's choices. Furthermore, it has no variability, unlike K Means and we do not expect the clustering to differ if we repeat the clustering procedure

On the other hand, it requires the user to choose the desired degree of granularity of clustering

A comparison of 2 clustering algorithms¶

Two clustering algorithms were applied:

  • 1 - K Means
  • 2 - Hierarchical Clustering (HC)

with the purpose to split the customers of our dataset into groups that may have a palpable business meaning. K Means forced us to choose the number of clusters a priori, and to minimize the arbitrarity of such decision we adopted the so called elbow method, which however is not univocous. Three groups seemed like the best choice. HC does not require users to choose the number of clusters a priori and is not subject to variability, however, users still get to a point where they will have to decide how fine grained they want their clustering to be. For the sake of comparability, 3 groups were chosen, and the silhouette index confirmed this to be an equally good clustering. Both algorithms, when 3 groups are identified, yield comparable results

Business implications¶

These clustering algorithms suggest that customers could be split into 3 groups:

    1. Affluent customers, with a large credit and many credit cards
    1. Average customers, who have an average or above average number of credit cards at their disposal
    1. Customers with lower credit (hence possibly lower income), and few credit cards available

While these 3 profiles may not exhaust the whole array of customer personalities that banks routinely encounter in their daily activities, our analysis, based on the provided data was able to carve out these profiles

These customers differ in their habits.

Group 1 pays rare visits to bank offices, while preferring their interactions with their bankers to occurr online. They might be well educated professionals, who are confortable using home banking websites and conducting their operations over the computer. The bank should probably make sure that their home banking system responds to the needs of such customers (e.g. offering online investment opportunities, stock purchase, financial instruments, private banking and custom offering etc)

Group 2 is made up of average credit customers, who use credit cards and actually often own more than 1 card. They do not rely so much on remote interactions with their bank, but prefer to show up and talk to bank representatives. The bank should probably make sure that their branches are geared toward this kind of customer, maybe having consultants for loans, mortgages, saving accounts or low-risk investment.

Group 3 comprises customers with low credit and maybe 1 or few credit cards. These customers mostly call their banks over the phone. They might be lower-income, less educated customers (maybe elderly), who are more familiar with phone calls than internet. The bank should make sure to make phone services accessible to these customers, e.g. paying extra attention to clear menus if automated services are used and having consultants who are knowledgeable with issues that may be of interest to this segment, e.g. debt consolidation, small short term loans.
